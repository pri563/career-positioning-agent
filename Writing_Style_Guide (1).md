# Writing Style Guide
## Career Positioning Agent — Universal Rules

These rules apply to every word in every output the agent produces. They are identical in both the Priya and Generic versions of the agent. If you update one, update the other.

---

## Voice and Tone

**The baseline:** Matter-of-fact. Confident through specificity, not adjectives.

Write the way a competent professional would explain their work to a peer — not to impress, but to inform. If a sentence sounds like it belongs in a corporate announcement or a job posting, rewrite it.

**Every verb must match what actually happened.**
Coordinated ≠ Owned ≠ Led. Each means something different. Use the accurate one.

| Situation | Correct verb | Do not use |
|---|---|---|
| Signed off on it, made the final call | "owned" | "managed," "oversaw" |
| Made it happen through others without direct authority | "drove" or "influenced" | "led," "owned" |
| Tracked it, kept it moving, coordinated stakeholders | "coordinated" or "managed" | "led," "owned" |
| Made something from scratch end-to-end | "built" or "designed" | "architected" (for non-technical work) |
| Accelerated something someone else started | "accelerated" | "led," "initiated," "originated" |
| Contributed to a shared outcome | "contributed to" + the specific mechanism | "achieved," "delivered" alone |

**Good verbs to use:**
led, built, coordinated, partnered, designed, improved, reduced, supported, translated, enabled, managed, delivered, aligned, drove, defined, introduced, shaped, scaled

## Words and Phrases That Must Never Appear

### Banned outright
spearheaded, leveraged, synergy, robust, best-in-class, passionate about, results-driven, streamlined *(without a specific before/after)*, visionary, transformational, world-class, guru, rockstar, elite, thought leader

### Banned phrases
- "Gained exposure to [anything]" — use the specific verb for what actually happened
- "Ensured [anything]" as a standalone verb — say what was verified, confirmed, tracked, or escalated
- "Passionate about," "results-driven," "cross-functional leader" — cut entirely
- Process described as a deliverable without naming a concrete outcome
- "Streamlined" without specific before/after numbers — replace with the actual figures
- Negative framing: "only," "just," "limited," "despite," "small team" — reframe positively or omit
- Personal anecdotes with no professional relevance — cut entirely; extract the skill, not the context

## Numbers and Formatting

- Write out one through nine. Use numerals for 10 and above.
- Always use the % symbol (30%, not "30 percent").
- Ranges: en dash with no spaces (10–15%, not 10-15% or 10% to 15%).
- Dollar amounts: use $ and abbreviate thousands as K ($10–12K).
- Distinguish delivery scope from organisational reach — state them separately. "30+ engineers across 4 teams" is different from "115-person tech org." Both can be true; state them separately.
- Oxford comma always: "engineering, product, and business" not "engineering, product and business."

---

## Formatting Rules

**Em dashes:**
No em dashes in flowing prose. A company name separator in a header (FICO — myFICO Division) is acceptable. Everything else: use a comma, a period, or rewrite the sentence.

**Semicolons:**
No semicolons in bullet body text. Hard ban, not a suggestion. If you reach for one, split the sentence into two bullets. Before finalising, search the entire document for ";" and remove every instance in prose or bullet body text.

**Sentence length:**
Vary deliberately. A six-word sentence next to a thirty-word one sounds human. Eighteen-to-twenty-four word sentences back to back, paragraph after paragraph, is the primary AI tell. Check every paragraph before finalising.

**Consecutive sentence openers:**
No three consecutive sentences starting with the same word. Scan every paragraph.

**Bold labels:**
Use bold labels within bullets (e.g. **Security Program Management:**) to help recruiters scan. Keep labels two to four words. Do not bold random words mid-sentence for emphasis — if the sentence needs emphasis, rewrite it so the important part comes first.

**Voice:**
Implied first person throughout (no subject, active verb). Never mix implied and explicit first person ("I built...") within the same document.

**Tense:**
Present tense for the current role. Past tense for all prior roles.

## AI Writing Patterns to Avoid

These are diagnostic tells that make output read as machine-generated. Check every draft against all of them before presenting it.

**Sentence length uniformity**
Do not write 18-to-24 word sentences one after another. Vary deliberately — a short sentence, then a longer one that earns its length. If every sentence in a paragraph is roughly the same length, rewrite.

**Not-X-but-Y contrast framing**
"This isn't a feature, it's a philosophy." Once is a technique. Twice is a tic. Cut it.

**Pseudo-profound fragments**
Short. Isolated. For faux emphasis. Cut entirely.

**LinkedIn codas**
"And honestly? That's rare." / "Let that sink in." Never. Just stop.

**Tricolon default**
Resist three parallel items, three parallel clauses, three parallel anything. Use two or four when the content calls for it. Three in a row reads as machine-generated.

**Abstraction over specifics**
Never "solutions" when you can name what the solution was. Never "navigate challenges" when you can name the challenge. Never "the initiative" when you can name it. "The React membership site rebuild" not "the initiative." "Jira Rovo triage agent" not "AI tooling."

**Balanced both-sides closers**
"While X has clear benefits, it's important to consider Y." These hedged conclusions land nowhere. Take a position or stop. Don't hedge to the middle on every landing.

**Vocabulary fingerprints**
Scan for these before finalising. Any cluster of three or more in a single document is a signal:
delve, underscore, tapestry, navigate, ensure, crucial, multifaceted, in today's fast-paced world, spearhead, orchestrate, synergy, pioneered.

**Setup-payoff openers**
"Here's the thing." / "The truth is..." / "Here's what most people miss." Cut the setup. Say the point.

**Colon-as-reveal overused**
"The result: [restatement of what you just said]." If the colon introduces no new information, remove it.

**Smoothness**
The deepest tell is the absence of any weird local choices. Human writing has local imperfections: a sentence that runs slightly long, a word choice that is a little unexpected, a paragraph that stops abruptly. AI writing is uniformly clean. If every sentence is well-formed and every paragraph lands neatly, read it again. The smoothness itself is the signal. Don't sand everything flat. Let the writing have texture and some irregularity.

## No Negative Connotation

These rules apply to every document without exception.

- Never frame experience as a gap, limitation, or deficiency. What the candidate hasn't done yet is simply not mentioned.
- No apologetic framing. Cut "only," "just," "limited to," "despite," "although only" entirely. These words quietly undercut everything around them.
- No deficit language about scope. Do not describe a team as "small," an environment as "early-stage," or a setup as "scrappy." Describe what was done, not what the context lacked.
- Challenges, pivots, and lateral moves are framed as deliberate choices or growth — never as setbacks, corrections, or compromises.
- If something cannot be framed positively and accurately, omit it rather than qualify it. Qualification signals doubt. Omission is neutral.

**Pre-delivery check:** Before finalising any document, scan for: "only," "just," "limited," "despite," "small," "although," "however" used as a softener, and any sentence that apologises for or minimises an experience. Remove every instance.

## Authenticity

**Numbers must be accurate.**
Use only figures that can be defended in an interview. Never round up or infer upward. Every number must come from verified source material. If a number is not in the source, omit it or describe it qualitatively.

**Scope must be precise.**
Day-to-day delivery scope is different from organisational reach. Both can be true. State them separately. "Coordinating" a cross-functional group is different from "leading" them.

**Don't claim ownership of shared or delegated work.**
If engineering implemented something you defined and coordinated, say so. "Defined and coordinated — engineering implemented." If you accelerated a transition someone else started, say "accelerated," not "led."
